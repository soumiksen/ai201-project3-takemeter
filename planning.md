# AI201 Milestone 1 — Label Taxonomy

## Community
r/television

## Observed Patterns
r/television posts fall into recognizable patterns very quickly. The most substantive posts compare a show's choices to specific narrative or cinematic techniques — they name episodes, cite writing decisions, or draw parallels to other works. A second large category is confident declarative statements ("X show is overrated," "the writing fell off in season 3") with no supporting argument — the opinion is the whole post. A third pattern is pure emotional reaction: posts tied to a specific episode airing recently, expressing shock, grief, or hype with little analytical content. Regulars in the community clearly distinguish between "actual TV criticism" and "just vibes," and frequently call out posts that mistake confidence for analysis. The boundary between a developed opinion and an undeveloped one is the most contested line in the sub.

## Label Taxonomy

### `critique`
**Definition:** The post makes a structured argument about a show's writing, direction, acting, or narrative structure, supported by specific references to episodes, scenes, or comparisons to other works.

**Clear example 1:** 'The Sopranos finale works because the cut to black mirrors Tony's entire arc — we've always been inside his paranoid POV, and now we're cut off from it just like he is. Compare this to the Pine Barrens episode where ambiguity is used for comedy; here it's existential dread.'

**Clear example 2:** 'Succession season 4 deliberately slows its pacing after episode 3 — every scene with the siblings runs about 20% longer than in prior seasons, which makes the power vacuum feel physically uncomfortable to sit in.'

**Uncertain example:** 'Better Call Saul is better than Breaking Bad because it trusts its audience more and the cinematography is more intentional.' — Makes a claim with some specificity (cinematography, audience trust) but doesn't actually demonstrate it with scenes or evidence.

### `hot_take`
**Definition:** The post asserts a bold or contrarian opinion about a show's quality, characters, or cultural standing without providing reasoning or evidence — the claim itself is the entire content.

**Clear example 1:** 'Game of Thrones was never actually good. Seasons 1–4 are just as hollow as 7–8, people just didn't notice yet.'

**Clear example 2:** 'Unpopular opinion: The Office should have ended at season 3. Everything after is a different, worse show.'

**Uncertain example:** 'The Wire season 2 is underrated because it takes the show's institutional critique into an entirely different sector of Baltimore society.' — Has a reason, but it's essentially a restatement of the premise rather than an argument. Borderline.

### `reaction`
**Definition:** The post expresses an immediate emotional response to a specific episode or scene that just aired, with little or no analytical content — the post is capturing a feeling in the moment.

**Clear example 1:** 'I cannot believe they just killed [character]. I am not okay. That finale destroyed me.'

**Clear example 2:** 'Just finished the White Lotus season 3 finale. Absolutely floored. Cannot stop thinking about that last shot.'

**Uncertain example:** 'That episode of Severance just broke my brain — the way they flipped the innie/outie structure in the last 10 minutes is genuinely the most inventive TV I've seen in years.' — Emotional tone but gestures at a specific structural observation. Could be reaction or critique.

## Mutual Exclusivity Check
The most likely overlap is between critique and hot_take when a post has confident, specific-sounding language but no actual argument. Decision rule: if you removed the opinion framing entirely, would the remaining content still constitute a reason to believe the claim? If yes → critique. If the "evidence" is just a restatement of the opinion in different words → hot_take.

The second overlap is between hot_take and reaction when someone reacts to a recent episode with a strong declarative opinion ("this show is finished after that episode"). Decision rule: if the post is primarily tied to a specific airing event and the opinion is clearly triggered by that moment → reaction. If the opinion is generalized beyond the episode and presented as a standing verdict on the show → hot_take.

critique and reaction rarely overlap because critique requires distance and structure that raw reactions don't have. The Severance uncertain example above is the edge case: label it reaction if the analytical observation is brief and embedded in emotional language, critique only if the structural point is developed with supporting detail.

## Community Description
r/television is a large general TV discussion community where posts range from rigorous episodic analysis to impulsive reactions to whatever just aired. I distinguish between three types of discourse: critique (structured arguments backed by specific scene or narrative references), hot_take (confident declarative opinions with no supporting reasoning), and reaction (immediate emotional responses tied to a specific episode). This taxonomy matters because r/television members actively value the difference between genuine criticism and confident noise — the community's own vocabulary around "actually good posts" maps closely onto these distinctions.
